Clinamen
 

About

The universe and everything in it, including mankind, are the subject of the epic poem on The Nature of Things, which had been written in Latin in dactylic hexameter around 59 and 55 BCE. He is the main source for comprehending Epicurean physics, which argues that atoms freely move throughout space and fall to the ground as a result of their weight. But occasionally, at unpredictable times and locations, the eternal, universal fall of the atoms is disrupted by a very small deviation dubbed the “Clinamen” (2.216 - 292), thus according Lucretius. All natural things, such as the realm of compound bodies, are produced by the ensuing vortex. ( For Gilles Deleuze incertus does not mean indeterminate, but rather unassignable. It occurs in an interval smaller than the minimum of continuous, thinkable time.)

The word Clinamen can also signify “swerve.” Thus according to Lucretius, the Clinamen is responsible for both natural and mental creations. Michel Serres contends that it shouldn’t be viewed as a sporadic “random” occurrence but rather as the theoretical manifestation of an unfathomably intricate order of events. It is also the origin of free will in Lucretius view. Because of the minute swerving of the atoms at no specific moment or location, “the mind itself has no intrinsic necessity to decide its every act and drive it to suffer in helpless passivity.” (2.292)

We can make the argument that the atoms are the images, texts, videos, sounds, etc... that exist on the internet (the universe) and that they are also in constant motion, causing slight variations or Clinamen that result in the variety of the universe as we know it, creating infinite variations of new “atoms” in a never-ending cycle. This is similar to how it is argued like in The Nature of Things.

In the texts that follow, we’ll examine how this process impacts various cultural practices, especially art, by making appropriation or “imitating” a central modernist cultural strategy.
